A Policy Framework for Accountable Urban Governance in Mulund

I. Introduction: Growth Without Governance

Mulund stands at a critical urban juncture. Once perceived as a relatively planned suburban extension, it is now experiencing the cumulative pressures of accelerated real estate development, strained infrastructure, and uneven civic prioritisation.

The issue is not growth per se. Cities must evolve. However, when expansion outpaces institutional capacity and planning coherence, the consequences manifest as congestion, flooding, reduced liveability, and declining public trust.

"In a city governed by one of the wealthiest municipal bodies in the country, why do basic civic outcomes remain inconsistent?"

II. The Structural Problem

Across Mulund, there is a visible disconnect between capital-intensive urban development and foundational civic provisioning.

High-density construction continues to be sanctioned even as drainage systems remain outdated, road capacities are exceeded, and pedestrian infrastructure is neglected.

III. Policy Priorities

1. Regulating Growth

Policy Direction:

  • A temporary moratorium on new high-density building approvals until critical infrastructure benchmarks are met.
  • Permissions contingent upon drainage capacity, water supply, and road network upgrades.
Rationale: Infrastructure must precede, not follow, density.

2. Reclaiming Public Space

Policy Direction:

  • Creation of continuous, obstruction-free pedestrian corridors.
  • Scientifically planned hawking zones based on footfall data and spatial mapping.
Rationale: Economic activity should not compromise basic mobility rights.

3. Equitable Urban Planning (PAP)

Policy Direction:

  • Advocacy for a decentralised city-wide PAP rehabilitation framework.
  • Mandatory infrastructure readiness assessments prior to any relocation.
Rationale: Urban inclusion cannot come at the cost of creating new zones of stress.

4. Fiscal Transparency

Policy Direction:

  • Publication of accessible ward-level expenditure reports.
  • Project tracking dashboards with timelines and status updates.
Rationale: Transparency is foundational to public trust.

IV. Governance Reform

Beyond sectoral policies, we propose a reimagining of the interface between citizens and governance:

  • Regular, structured public accountability forums at the ward level.
  • Digitised and accessible civic reporting systems.
  • Institutional mechanisms for citizen feedback.

V. Conclusion

Mulund’s challenges are symptomatic of a broader urban condition—where governance struggles to keep pace with growth. What is required is consistent, principled administration rooted in planning discipline, equity, and transparency.
