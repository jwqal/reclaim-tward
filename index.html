Reclaim T Ward: A Policy Framework for Accountable Urban Governance in Mulund

Introduction Structural Problem Policy Priorities Governance Reform Conclusion

I. Introduction: Growth Without Governance

Mulund stands at a critical urban juncture. Once perceived as a relatively planned suburban extension, it is now experiencing the cumulative pressures of accelerated real estate development, strained infrastructure, and uneven civic prioritisation.

The issue is not growth per se. Cities must evolve. However, when expansion outpaces institutional capacity and planning coherence, the consequences manifest as congestion, flooding, reduced liveability, and declining public trust.

This document does not present a catalogue of electoral assurances. Instead, it outlines a policy-oriented framework grounded in three core principles: infrastructural adequacy, spatial fairness, and administrative accountability.

At its core lies a simple question: In a city governed by one of the wealthiest municipal bodies in the country, why do basic civic outcomes remain inconsistent?

II. The Structural Problem: A Misalignment of Priorities

Across Mulund, there is a visible disconnect between:

  • Capital-intensive urban development, and
  • Foundational civic provisioning

High-density construction continues to be sanctioned even as:

  • Drainage systems remain outdated
  • Road capacities are exceeded
  • Pedestrian infrastructure is neglected

This reflects not merely administrative delay, but a deeper prioritisation imbalance, where approvals and expansion precede systemic readiness.

Correcting this requires a shift from reactive governance to anticipatory planning.

III. Policy Priorities

1. Regulating Growth: Aligning Development with Infrastructure Capacity

Policy Direction:

  • A temporary moratorium on new high-density building approvals in Mulund until critical infrastructure benchmarks are met
  • Establishment of infrastructure-linked clearance criteria, wherein permissions are contingent upon:
    • Drainage capacity upgrades
    • Water supply augmentation
    • Road network decongestion

Rationale: Urban development must be sequenced, not simultaneous. Infrastructure must precede, not follow, density.

2. Reclaiming Public Space: Pedestrian Rights and Informal Economies

Policy Direction:

  • Creation of continuous, obstruction-free pedestrian corridors in critical transit zones
  • Implementation of scientifically planned hawking zones, based on:
    • Footfall data
    • Spatial mapping
    • Stakeholder consultation

Rationale: The objective is not eviction, but orderly coexistence, where economic activity does not compromise basic mobility rights.

3. Equitable Urban Planning: Rethinking PAP Relocation

Policy Direction:

  • Advocacy for a decentralised and city-wide PAP rehabilitation framework
  • Mandatory infrastructure readiness assessments prior to any relocation decision
  • Integration of PAP housing within broader urban planning, rather than as isolated clusters

Rationale: Urban inclusion cannot come at the cost of creating new zones of infrastructural stress. Equity must be both social and spatial.

4. Fiscal Transparency: Bridging the Accountability Deficit

Policy Direction:

  • Publication of ward-level expenditure reports in accessible formats
  • Introduction of project tracking dashboards with timelines and status updates
  • Reprioritisation of spending towards:
    • Critical traffic bottlenecks
    • Public healthcare infrastructure

Rationale: Transparency is not merely informational—it is foundational to public trust and participatory governance.

5. Mobility and Connectivity: Addressing Chronic Congestion

Policy Direction:

  • Time-bound acceleration of:
    • Development Plan (DP) road linkages
    • Strategic infrastructure projects such as the Goregaon-Mulund Link Road
  • Institutionalisation of monthly public progress disclosures

Rationale: Urban mobility must be treated as an economic and social priority, not a residual concern.

IV. Governance Reform: From Representation to Accountability

Beyond sectoral policies, there is a need to reimagine the interface between citizens and governance structures.

  • Regular, structured public accountability forums at the ward level
  • Digitised and accessible civic reporting systems
  • Institutional mechanisms for citizen feedback integration in decision-making

This represents a shift from episodic electoral engagement to continuous civic participation.

V. Conclusion: Towards a More Coherent Urban Future

Mulund’s challenges are neither unique nor insurmountable. They are symptomatic of a broader urban condition—where governance struggles to keep pace with growth.

What is required is not extraordinary intervention, but consistent, principled administration rooted in:

  • Planning discipline
  • Equity
  • Transparency

Ultimately, the question is not whether change is possible, but whether governance is willing to prioritise long-term public outcomes over short-term expediency.
