Context

Growth Without Governance

"The issue is not growth per se. Cities must evolve. But when expansion outpaces planning coherence, liveability declines."

Mulund stands at a critical urban juncture. Once perceived as a relatively planned suburban extension, it is now experiencing the cumulative pressures of accelerated real estate development, strained infrastructure, and uneven civic prioritisation.

When expansion outpaces institutional capacity and planning coherence, the consequences manifest as congestion, flooding, reduced liveability, and declining public trust.

This document does not present a catalogue of electoral assurances. Instead, it outlines a policy-oriented framework grounded in three core principles: infrastructural adequacy, spatial fairness, and administrative accountability.

At its core lies a simple question: In a city governed by one of the wealthiest municipal bodies in the country, why do basic civic outcomes remain inconsistent?

The Problem

A Misalignment
of Priorities

Capital-intensive Urban Development

High-density construction continues to be sanctioned even as foundational civic systems buckle under pressure. Approvals and expansion precede systemic readiness.

Foundational Civic Neglect

Drainage systems remain outdated. Road capacities are exceeded. Pedestrian infrastructure is neglected. This reflects not administrative delay alone, but a deeper prioritisation imbalance.

Correcting this requires a shift from reactive governance to anticipatory planning.

Policy Framework

Five Priority
Directions

01
Regulation
Regulating Growth: Aligning Development with Infrastructure Capacity

Unregulated vertical expansion in Mulund has intensified pressure on already fragile civic systems.

Policy Direction

  • A temporary moratorium on new high-density building approvals until critical infrastructure benchmarks are met
  • Infrastructure-linked clearance criteria — permissions contingent on drainage capacity upgrades
  • Water supply augmentation and road network decongestion as prerequisites

Urban development must be sequenced, not simultaneous. Infrastructure must precede, not follow, density.

02
Public Space
Reclaiming Public Space: Pedestrian Rights and Informal Economies

The erosion of pedestrian space in Mulund — particularly in high-footfall zones such as areas surrounding the railway station — reflects a broader governance gap. The current approach has produced a zero-sum conflict between pedestrian mobility and informal livelihoods.

Policy Direction

  • Creation of continuous, obstruction-free pedestrian corridors in critical transit zones
  • Scientifically planned hawking zones based on footfall data
  • Spatial mapping and stakeholder consultation as standard process

The objective is not eviction, but orderly coexistence — where economic activity does not compromise basic mobility rights.

03
Spatial Equity
Equitable Urban Planning: Rethinking PAP Relocation

The concentration of Project Affected Person (PAP) housing in specific localities — including parts of Mulund East — raises important questions of spatial equity and planning justice.

Policy Direction

  • Advocacy for a decentralised, city-wide PAP rehabilitation framework
  • Mandatory infrastructure readiness assessments prior to any relocation decision
  • Integration of PAP housing within broader urban planning, not as isolated clusters

Urban inclusion cannot come at the cost of creating new zones of infrastructural stress. Equity must be both social and spatial.

04
Transparency
Fiscal Transparency: Bridging the Accountability Deficit

A recurring concern in municipal governance is the opacity surrounding expenditure. While allocations may be substantial, their translation into visible, high-impact outcomes remains uneven.

Policy Direction

  • Publication of ward-level expenditure reports in accessible formats
  • Introduction of project tracking dashboards with timelines and status updates
  • Reprioritisation of spending towards critical traffic bottlenecks and public healthcare infrastructure

Transparency is not merely informational — it is foundational to public trust and participatory governance.

05
Mobility
Mobility & Connectivity: Addressing Chronic Congestion

Mulund's connectivity challenges — particularly along key arterial routes — are not new, but have intensified due to population and vehicular growth.

Policy Direction

  • Time-bound acceleration of Development Plan (DP) road linkages
  • Strategic infrastructure projects such as the Goregaon–Mulund Link Road
  • Institutionalisation of monthly public progress disclosures

Urban mobility must be treated as an economic and social priority, not a residual concern.

Civic Interface

From Representation
to Accountability

Beyond sectoral policies, there is a need to reimagine the interface between citizens and governance structures — a shift from episodic electoral engagement to continuous civic participation.

◎
Public Accountability Forums
Regular, structured forums at the ward level where citizens engage directly with elected representatives on outcomes, not promises.
⊞
Digitised Civic Reporting
Accessible, digitised systems for civic issue reporting with transparent status tracking available to every resident.
◈
Citizen Feedback Integration
Institutional mechanisms ensuring citizen feedback is formally integrated into planning and decision-making processes.
Towards A More Coherent Urban Future

Consistent, Principled
Administration.

Mulund's challenges are neither unique nor insurmountable. They are symptomatic of a broader urban condition — where governance struggles to keep pace with growth.

The question is not whether change is possible, but whether governance is willing to prioritise long-term public outcomes over short-term expediency.
